Mars - The Red Planet

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and is often called the "Red Planet" due to its reddish appearance, which is caused by iron oxide (rust) on its surface.

Key Facts about Mars

  • Diameter: Approximately 6,779 km
  • Mass: About 0.107 times that of Earth
  • Surface Area: Roughly 28% of Earth's surface area
  • Atmosphere: Composed mainly of carbon dioxide (95.97%), with traces of nitrogen and argon
  • Moons: Two small moons, Phobos and Deimos
  • Gravity: About 38% of Earth's gravity
  • Day Length: A Martian day (sol) is approximately 24.6 hours
  • Year Length: A Martian year is about 687 Earth days

ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation)

India's Mangalyaan (Mars Orbiter Mission) made ISRO the first Asian agency to reach Mars orbit successfully. It studied Martian surface and atmosphere and took stunning full-disk images of Mars.

NASA (USA)

NASA's Perseverance and Curiosity rovers have been exploring the Martian surface, analyzing soil samples and looking for signs of past microbial life. Discoveries like the Cheyava Falls rock containing organic molecules suggest Mars might once have supported life.

ESA (European Space Agency)

ESA's ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter studies atmospheric gases like methane, which might indicate biological or geological activity. The mission also works with Roscosmos to search for subsurface water.

Roscosmos (Russia)

Russia has a long history with Mars missions. Though early attempts had mixed success, it now collaborates on missions like ExoMars with ESA to explore subsurface environments and plan for future Mars landers.

CNSA (China National Space Administration)

China's Tianwen-1 mission made a major leap by sending both an orbiter and a lander (with the Zhurong rover) in 2021. The rover is actively exploring the Utopia Planitia region, studying soil, climate, and magnetic fields.

JAXA (Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency)

While JAXA has not yet landed a rover on Mars, it contributes to international collaborations and is planning future missions related to Martian moons (Phobos and Deimos) under its MMX (Martian Moons eXploration) project.

Deimos – The Smaller Martian Moon

Deimos is the smaller and outermost of Mars’ two moons. It is only about 12 km in diameter and is covered in a thick layer of dust. Deimos has a smoother appearance than Phobos and orbits Mars approximately every 30 hours. Scientists believe it may be a captured asteroid from the outer asteroid belt.

Phobos – The Closer Martian Moon

Phobos is the larger and closer of the two Martian moons, with a diameter of about 22 km. It orbits Mars in just 7.6 hours — faster than Mars rotates. Phobos is heavily cratered and is slowly spiraling toward Mars. It is expected to either crash into the planet or break apart into a ring in the next 50 million years.
